Feature Scaling

Suppose we have some sample of data xˉ(1),xˉ(2),…,xˉ(n){\bar{x}^{(1)},\bar{x}^{(2)},\ldots,\bar{x}^{(n)}}xˉ(1),xˉ(2),…,xˉ(n) for some integer nnn drawn from some natural source.

Typically for all i=1,2,…,ni =1,2,\ldots,ni=1,2,…,n, xˉ(i)∈Rd\bar{x}^{(i)} \in \mathbb{R}^dxˉ(i)∈Rd for some d∈Nd \in \mathbb{N}d∈N.

The integer ddd is the dimension of the data.

The xˉ(i)\bar{x}^{(i)}xˉ(i) might be virtually anything. Some examples...

  • Pictures of cats

  • Measurements of shellfish parts

  • Measurements of flower parts

  • etc

We think of the data as being arranged in one large matrix XXX with nnn rows and ddd columns. The iiith row of XXX is the data point xˉ(i)\bar{x}^{(i)}xˉ(i). (Because all vectors are column vectors by default, the iiith row of XXX is technically xˉ(i)T\bar{x}^{(i)T}xˉ(i)T, the transpose of xˉ(i)\bar{x}^{(i)}xˉ(i).)

Usually we have n>>dn >> dn>>d and the matrix XXX is "tall and skinny".

Below an example is given of a matrix which has n=40n=40n=40 and d=2d=2d=2.

In the example XXX is made up of artificial data.

The columns of XXX are called features. For example suppose the xˉ(i)\bar{x}^{(i)}xˉ(i) are triples of real numbers representing the radius,weight and color of a metal sphere. Then the columns of XXX would refer to the radius,weight and color of the spheres, respectively, and the "features" of the data would be radius, weight and color.

Often features are naturally on different scales. For example if the metal balls are made of some very light substance and radius is measured in cm but weight is measured in kg it might happen that the numbers in the weight column are thousands of times smaller than the numbers in the radius column.

For many (but not all) machine learning algorithms, this mismatch of scale makes problems.

Therefore we are interested in ways to do feature scaling. This means transforming XXX so that all of the columns are on the same scale.

In the example below, notice that in its raw form, XXX is quite skinny and tall. We want to scale the features so that it becomes more "roundish".

In the notebook below we explore four methods for doing feature scaling. All are explained in this Wikipedia article on feature scaling.

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
def plotX(X,title):
    plt.scatter(X[:,0],X[:,1])
    plt.title(title)
    center = X.mean(axis=0)
    maxC = (X-center).max()
    plt.xlim([center[0]-maxC,center[0]+maxC])
    plt.ylim([center[1]-maxC,center[1]+maxC])
    plt.show()
plotX(X,"Data before feature scaling")
